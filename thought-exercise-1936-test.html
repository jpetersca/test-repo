The 1936 Test

Lightlabs Advisory January 2026

Imagine you are living in 1926. Now, imagine if just 10 years later, in 1936, you were handed all the key innovations of 2026.

This is the premise behind a prediction from Demis Hassabis, the CEO of Google DeepMind. He suggested that over the next decade, AI will drive 10x the innovation at 10x the speed. The mathematical and societal implication is one hundred years of progress condensed into the next ten.(1)

Let's ground that in our 1926 experiment. In that year, the television was a mechanical, grainy marvel being demonstrated on a one-inch screen. A century of progress would mean that by 1936, the concept wouldn't have just evolved to a clearer picture tube in a heavy wooden box; it would have leaped to a 120-inch, 8K flexible screen with immersive sound.

Consider one more example. In 1926, horses still outnumbered cars in the US. The "100-year leap" wouldn't just mean more Fords on the road; it would mean a Tesla Cybertruck on a dusty road in 1936, silently gliding past a horse-drawn cart.

This is the scale of change we are being asked to contemplate. Anyone who claims to know what our world will look like in 3 to 5 years is not merely wrong, they are also incorrect.

The Commodification of Cognition

For centuries, our economy has been built on the value of specialized knowledge. Synthesis, for example, was a highly valued white-collar skill. A junior law associate summarizing hundreds of documents of case law was performing a difficult, expensive cognitive task. An LLM now performs this task in minutes, if not seconds.(2)

What happens when AI can not only synthesize all existing medical research but also generate novel, testable hypotheses to cure diseases? What happens when it can not only code a website but build entire software systems based on a simple conversation? What is the value of a strategist when an AI can model a million competitive scenarios in a minute?

The Central Question

We are facing the commodification of intelligence itself. When the cost of sophisticated intellectual labour trends toward zero, much of what our society has assigned value to disappears.

The economy will be transformed into something unrecognizable, likely centred on the few things AI cannot replicate: genuine human experience, physical presence, and moral courage.
